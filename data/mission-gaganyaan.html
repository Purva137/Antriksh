G1 Status · Apr 30, 2026 · Final Pad Integration · Launch Date Expected Early May

Gaganyaan-G1
Uncrewed Test Flight

As of April 30, 2026, the G1 mission has entered final pad integration. ISRO Chairperson Dr. V. Narayanan confirmed on April 8 that the mission is 90% complete. Integration of Vyommitra onto the spacecraft was completed on April 28, 2026. Following this, the G1 Mission Readiness Review (MRR) was formally cleared on April 29, 2026 — the official green light for launch pad rollout. The official launch date announcement is expected in early May.

G1 will test the full mission profile from launch to splashdown. Key systems under validation include the ablative heat shield performance during re-entry at ~8 km/s, the parachute deployment system (already tested at 600 km/h on a rocket sled at TBRL, Chandigarh), and the crew module structural integrity under qualification-level aerodynamic loads.

Recovery will be led by the Indian Navy and Indian Coast Guard in the Arabian Sea off Gujarat's coast. For G1, ISRO is also deploying chartered ships equipped with specialized tracking terminals in the Pacific and North Atlantic Oceans — ensuring continuous data monitoring and telemetry coverage during the uncrewed re-entry phase across all global corridors.

Overview

India Goes to Space

Gaganyaan — from Sanskrit: gagana (celestial) + yāna (vehicle) — is India's first human spaceflight programme. The mission will carry three Indian astronauts to low Earth orbit at 400 km altitude. The initial H1 mission is planned for 3 days, with the vehicle designed to support up to 7 days in orbit — leaving room to extend future missions as India's human spaceflight experience grows.

When it launches, India will become only the fourth nation in history to independently send humans to space — after the Soviet Union, the United States, and China. No other country has done it. And India will do it with a budget smaller than most Hollywood blockbusters.

The programme was formally sanctioned in 2009 and commissioned in 2017 with an initial budget of ₹10,000 crore — since revised to ₹20,000 crore to cover the expanded scope including the Bharatiya Antariksh Station goals. It represents not just a milestone, but a proof of concept — that India can do anything in space it sets its mind to.

Why Gaganyaan Matters
India will become the 4th nation ever to independently launch humans to space. The mission validates India's complete human spaceflight ecosystem — from life support systems to crew escape mechanisms to recovery operations. Everything built in India, by Indians.

Gaganyatri Shubhanshu Shukla
In 2025, Group Captain Shubhanshu Shukla flew to the International Space Station on Axiom Space's Axiom-4 mission — becoming the first Indian to reach the ISS. He spent over 18 days in space, conducting microgravity experiments, and returned safely. This was India's dress rehearsal for Gaganyaan.
Astronaut Training Facility (ATF) — Bengaluru + Field Operations
All four astronauts train at ISRO's dedicated Astronaut Training Facility in Bengaluru — covering classroom academics, physical fitness, flight simulator training, microgravity familiarization via parabolic flights, aero-medical training, survival drills, and full crew procedures. Yoga is also a formal part of the programme. Additional training was conducted in Russia at Gagarin Cosmonaut Training Centre.
Mission MITRA — Leh, Ladakh · April 2026
In April 2026, ISRO concluded Mission MITRA (Mapping of Interoperable Traits and Response Assessment) — a first-of-its-kind team behavioural study conducted in Leh, Ladakh, co-designed by ISRO and the IAF Institute of Aerospace Medicine (IAF-IAM). The exercise examined the physiological and psychological dynamics of the crew under extreme isolation, altitude, and environmental stress — specifically studying how the crew and mission control coordinate under pressure. Ladakh's thin air, sub-zero conditions, and terrain made it the closest Earth analogue to the hostile environment of space. No simulator can replicate this. Mission MITRA made sure the humans were ready before Vyommitra flies.
Looking Ahead — India's Expanding Astronaut Corps
ISRO has officially recommended a second batch of 10 astronauts consisting of 6 mission pilots (including both fighter and combat helicopter pilots, broadening selection beyond fighter pilots for the first time) and 4 civilian specialists from STEM fields. While military pilots will lead the early flights, civilian specialists are slated to begin joining mission crews from the fourth crewed Gaganyaan mission onward. The long-term vision is an astronaut cadre of up to 40 individuals — scientists, engineers, and pilots — making India's space programme one of the most diverse human spaceflight ecosystems in the world.
Uncrewed Test Pilot

Vyommitra

Before any human flies on Gaganyaan, India's half-humanoid robot — Vyommitra (from Sanskrit: vyoma = space + mitra = friend) — will make the journey first. Developed by ISRO, Vyommitra is specifically designed to fly on the G1 and G2 uncrewed test missions.

Vyommitra is not just a showpiece. She performs real mission-critical functions — operating switches and panels, monitoring the cabin environment, detecting system anomalies, and responding to queries. Her data directly informs what happens when humans fly.

Most critically, Vyommitra will test the life support system — the oxygen supply, CO₂ scrubbing, cabin pressure regulation, and temperature control — in the actual microgravity environment of orbit. No simulation on Earth can fully replicate this. Vyommitra does it for real, so the human crew doesn't have to find out the hard way.

She is also designed to detect and flag any off-nominal readings in the crew module systems — essentially acting as a co-pilot without a heartbeat.

Spacecraft

The Orbital Module

The Gaganyaan spacecraft — called the Orbital Module — consists of two parts: the Crew Module where astronauts live, and the Service Module that powers the mission.

The Crew Module is a double-walled pressurized capsule with an Earth-like environment for the crew. It houses crew interfaces, life support systems, avionics, and the deceleration system for re-entry.

The Service Module provides propulsion, thermal control, power, and deployment mechanisms while in orbit. It separates from the Crew Module before re-entry.

A critical safety feature is the Crew Escape System (CES) — high-burn-rate solid motors that can pull the Crew Module to safety within milliseconds if anything goes wrong during launch.

Equally important is the Flight Crew Emergency Egress System (FCEES) — a zipline-based rapid evacuation mechanism installed at the launch pad. If an emergency occurs before liftoff, astronauts can exit the tower at speed and reach a safe bunker in seconds. As of April 2026, ISRO has been actively highlighting and refining this ground-side safety infrastructure alongside the in-flight escape systems.

Launch Vehicle

HLVM3 — Human Rated LVM3

ISRO's most powerful rocket — the LVM3 — has been specially modified and human-rated for Gaganyaan. Every system has been redesigned to meet human safety standards, and it's been rechristened the Human Rated LVM3 (HLVM3).

The rocket consists of three stages: solid fuel strap-on boosters (S200), liquid propellant core stage (L110), and a cryogenic upper stage (C25) using liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen — the same technology used by the world's most powerful rockets.

The first human-rated Vikas engine for Gaganyaan was delivered by Godrej Enterprises Group in November 2025 — a milestone that shows how India's private sector is now part of the space journey.

Programme Timeline

The Road to Launch

2006–2007
Programme Begins
Preliminary studies for an "Orbital Vehicle" begin under ISRO. A simple capsule for 2 astronauts, 1 week endurance, splashdown landing.
Feb 2009
Government Sanctions Gaganyaan
Government of India officially approves the human spaceflight programme. India commits to sending its own astronauts to space.
Oct 2023
TV-D1 — Crew Escape Test Success
ISRO successfully tests the Crew Escape System at altitude — the most critical safety mechanism. The Crew Module was pulled to safety in a simulated abort scenario.
Dec 2024
ESA Signs Support Agreement
ESA agrees to provide ground station support across Europe for all Gaganyaan missions — ensuring continuous communication with the crew.
2025
Gaganyatri Shubhanshu Flies to ISS
Group Captain Shubhanshu Shukla flies to the ISS on Axiom-4. India's first astronaut on the ISS. 18 days in space. Microgravity experiments. A dress rehearsal for Gaganyaan.
Nov 2025
First Human-Rated Engine Delivered
Godrej Enterprises delivers the first human-rated L110 Vikas engine to ISRO. India's private sector officially joins the Gaganyaan journey.
2026–2027
Gaganyaan-G1 & G2 — Vyommitra Flies First
Two uncrewed test flights carrying Vyommitra, ISRO's half-humanoid robot. G1 validates heat shield re-entry, parachute systems, and life support. G2 completes the full mission profile. Only after both succeed will humans board Gaganyaan.
2027
Gaganyaan — India's First Crewed Mission
Three Indian astronauts launch on HLVM3. 400 km orbit. 3 days in space. Recovery in the Arabian Sea off Gujarat's coast by the Indian Navy and Coast Guard. India joins the elite club of human spaceflight nations — and begins building toward its own space station.

FAQ

Questions Answered

Q
When will Gaganyaan launch?
The crewed mission (Gaganyaan-H1) is currently targeted for 2028. Two uncrewed test missions will fly before humans board. Timelines may shift based on test results.
Q
Who will be on the crew?
ISRO has four astronaut-designates — all Indian Air Force pilots. The final three-person crew for the crewed mission has not been publicly announced yet.
Q
Where will they land?
The Crew Module will re-enter Earth's atmosphere and splash down in the Arabian Sea off India's west coast, where the Indian Navy will recover the crew and capsule.
Q
How can I watch the launch?
ISRO will livestream all Gaganyaan missions on their official YouTube channel and website. Antriksh will also carry the live stream when the time comes — stay tuned.
