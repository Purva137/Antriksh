Overview

The Day India Stunned the World

PSLV-C37 lifted off from SDSC SHAR, Sriharikota on February 15, 2017, at 9:28 AM IST. Over the next 28 minutes, it deployed 104 satellites into a 505 km sun-synchronous orbit — the highest number of satellites ever placed in orbit by a single rocket in a single mission, anywhere in history.

The previous record was 37 satellites, set by Russia in 2014. India nearly tripled it. The mission carried 3 Indian satellites — Cartosat-2D and two INS nano-satellites — along with 101 international satellites from 18 countries including the USA (96 satellites, mostly from Planet Labs), Israel, Kazakhstan, Netherlands, Switzerland, UAE, and others.

The launch was a commercial masterpiece: ISRO earned foreign exchange from 96 international payloads, demonstrated PSLV's reliability and precision to a global commercial market, and proved that India's launch vehicle could handle operational complexity far beyond standard missions.

Planet Labs — 88 Doves in One Shot
The bulk of the foreign payload was 88 Dove CubeSats from Planet Labs (USA) — tiny Earth-imaging satellites each the size of a shoebox. Planet Labs uses constellations of hundreds of these to photograph every point on Earth daily. PSLV-C37 deployed a significant portion of their constellation in a single pass. India was the commercial launch provider for one of the world's most ambitious satellite imaging networks.
The Engineering Challenge

28 Minutes of Precision

Deploying 104 satellites in a single pass is not just a matter of carrying them up — it is a precision choreography. Each satellite must be ejected at a specific velocity and angle so it enters its intended orbit without colliding with previously deployed satellites or the rocket itself.

ISRO engineers designed a deployment sequence where satellites were released in groups, with calculated time intervals and velocity increments between each release. The PSLV's fourth stage acted as the deployment platform, rotating and maneuvering between releases to ensure each satellite cleared the dispenser safely.

All 104 satellites were successfully placed in their target orbits. Not one collision. Not one failure. 28 minutes of flawless execution that took months of simulation and planning to design.

PSLV-C37 carried onboard video cameras that captured the entire deployment sequence — producing the now-famous footage of 101 nano-satellites streaming out in a swarm against the backdrop of Earth. The video was not just spectacular; it was operationally critical — the only way to visually verify that each small satellite had cleanly separated from the dispenser without tangling or collision, given the impossibility of ground-tracking 104 objects individually in real time.

The Collision Avoidance Problem
The greatest engineering challenge wasn't carrying 104 satellites — it was deploying them without any colliding with each other or the rocket. With relative velocities in the hundreds of metres per second, even a tiny error in deployment timing creates dangerous debris. ISRO's deployment sequence was calculated to millimetre precision. Zero incidents.
Legacy

What It Proved

🚀
PSLV's Commercial Dominance
PSLV-C37 cemented PSLV as the world's most reliable commercial small satellite launch vehicle. After this mission, international clients from 30+ nations chose PSLV. India earned hundreds of crores in foreign exchange from launching other nations' satellites.
🌍
India as Launch Superpower
No other nation had demonstrated the operational complexity of 104 simultaneous payloads. The mission positioned India as a tier-1 launch nation — not just capable of reaching orbit, but capable of doing so with commercial precision at scale.
📋
Record That Stood — Until SpaceX
India's record of 104 stood until SpaceX launched 143 satellites on Transporter-1 in January 2021 using rideshare. India held the world record for nearly 4 years. And the engineering principles ISRO proved — multi-satellite deployment precision — remain as relevant as ever.
🔬
Cartosat-2D — India's Own Science
Amid the 101 foreign satellites, India's own Cartosat-2D — a 0.65m resolution Earth observation satellite — was the mission's primary payload. Commercial revenue funded India's own science. The model was as elegant as the launch.
