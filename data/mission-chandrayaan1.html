Overview

India's First Step on the Moon

Chandrayaan-1 — from Sanskrit: chandra (moon) + yāna (vehicle) — was India's first lunar mission, launched on October 22, 2008, from SDSC SHAR on a PSLV-C11 rocket. It placed a 590 kg spacecraft into lunar orbit and conducted a comprehensive scientific study of the Moon over 312 days.

The mission carried 11 scientific instruments — 5 from ISRO and 6 from international partners including NASA, ESA, and Bulgaria. It was a global collaboration that produced globally significant science.

The crowning achievement came when NASA's Moon Mineralogy Mapper (M3) instrument aboard Chandrayaan-1 detected unambiguous spectroscopic evidence of water molecules and hydroxyl radicals on the lunar surface — particularly near the poles. This was one of the most significant lunar discoveries since the Apollo era.

The Water Discovery — Why It Changed Everything
Scientists had speculated about water ice at the Moon's poles for decades. Chandrayaan-1 confirmed it. This discovery transformed plans for future human lunar exploration — water on the Moon means future astronauts can potentially extract oxygen for air and hydrogen for fuel, making long-term lunar bases far more feasible.

Science Payloads

11 Instruments — A Global Mission

ISRO Instruments: Terrain Mapping Camera (TMC), Hyper Spectral Imager (HySI), Lunar Laser Ranging Instrument (LLRI), High Energy X-ray Spectrometer (HEX), Moon Impact Probe (MIP)

International Instruments (6 from 5 agencies): NASA — Moon Mineralogy Mapper (M3) and Miniature Synthetic Aperture Radar (Mini-SAR). ESA — C1XS (X-ray spectrometer) and SIR-2 (near-infrared spectrometer). Bulgaria (STIL) — RADOM radiation monitor. Germany (MPS) — contributed to C1XS detector development. Sweden (IRF) — contributed to the SIR-2 instrument. Five nations, one Indian spacecraft, one Moon.

International Collaboration
NASA, ESA, and Bulgaria contributed instruments to Chandrayaan-1 — making it one of the most internationally collaborative Indian space missions ever flown. The discovery of water came from a NASA instrument aboard an Indian spacecraft. Science has no borders.
Moon Impact Probe

India Touches the Moon — Nov 14, 2008

On November 14, 2008 — Jawaharlal Nehru's birthday — Chandrayaan-1 released the Moon Impact Probe (MIP), a small 34 kg probe that deliberately crashed into the lunar south pole at the Shackleton Crater. In the 25 minutes it took to descend, MIP captured video, measured the lunar atmosphere, and detected water vapour near the surface.

The MIP carried the Indian tricolour painted on its sides — making India one of a very small number of nations to have placed its flag on the Moon. The crash site was named Jawahar Point.

The Defining Discovery

Water on the Moon

The most significant discovery of Chandrayaan-1 — and one of the most important lunar discoveries since Apollo — was the detection of water molecules (H₂O) and hydroxyl radicals (OH) on the Moon's surface by NASA's Moon Mineralogy Mapper (M3).

The discovery was published in the journal Science in September 2009. The data showed that water molecules are concentrated near the lunar poles, in permanently shadowed regions where temperatures stay below −230°C — cold enough for water ice to survive for billions of years.

This confirmed decades of speculation and transformed the Moon from a dry, dead rock into a world with resources that could sustain future human exploration. Every lunar programme that followed — Artemis, Chandrayaan-3's south pole targeting, China's lunar plans — was influenced by what Chandrayaan-1 found.

Published in Science — Sep 2009
Three separate papers in the journal Science confirmed Chandrayaan-1's water discovery — one using M3 data, one using Cassini flyby data, and one using Deep Impact observations. Multiple independent confirmations. The discovery was real. India had changed lunar science forever.

Crystalline Feldspar — Confirming the Magma Ocean
Chandrayaan-1 detected large exposures of crystalline plagioclase feldspar in the lunar highlands — confirming the Global Magma Ocean Hypothesis. This theory holds that the early Moon was entirely molten, and as it cooled, lighter minerals like feldspar floated to the surface. India's data provided the global-scale evidence that finally confirmed it. A defining moment in understanding how the Moon itself formed.
🌋
Magmatic Water — From Inside the Moon
Beyond polar ice, Chandrayaan-1 discovered a separate phenomenon — magmatic water within the Bullialdus Crater. This water originated from the Moon's interior through volcanic processes billions of years ago. Two completely different types of water found on the Moon in one mission — a staggering scientific achievement that expanded our understanding of the Moon's geological history.
Mission Timeline

From Launch to Legacy

Oct 22, 2008
Launch — PSLV-C11
Chandrayaan-1 launches from SDSC SHAR at 6:22 AM IST. Perfect insertion into Earth transfer orbit.
Nov 8, 2008
Lunar Orbit Insertion
Chandrayaan-1 enters lunar orbit successfully. ISRO's first spacecraft around the Moon. India joins the lunar exploration club.
Nov 14, 2008
Moon Impact Probe Released
MIP separates and descends to the lunar south pole, impacting at Shackleton Crater. The impact site is named Jawahar Point. India's tricolour is on the Moon.
Nov 2008 – Aug 2009
Science Operations
TMC maps the lunar surface in 3D. M3 collects spectroscopic data. Mini-SAR looks for water ice. 312 days of continuous science.
May 19, 2009
Orbit Raised to 200 km
To better study the Moon's gravitational field and anomalies, ISRO raised Chandrayaan-1's orbit from 100 km to 200 km — providing a different vantage point for scientific instruments and reducing thermal stress on the spacecraft.
Apr 26, 2009
DC-DC Converter Failure — The First Domino
The spacecraft's DC-DC Converter failed on April 26, 2009 — the first critical hardware failure. This forced ISRO to switch to backup power systems, which ran hotter and placed additional thermal stress on the spacecraft's electronics. This was the first domino that ultimately cascaded into the star sensor failure three months later.
Aug 28, 2009
Star Sensor Failure — Mission Ends Early
Intense solar radiation caused the Star Sensors to fail — the instruments that gave the spacecraft its orientation reference. Without them, Chandrayaan-1 could not maintain attitude control. Communication was permanently lost. The mission ended 312 days in — planned for 2 years. The data already collected was more than enough to change history.
Sep 2009
Water Discovery Published
Three papers in Science confirm water on the Moon using Chandrayaan-1 data. India's name is forever written in the history of lunar science.
Legacy

What Chandrayaan-1 Started

💧
The Discovery That Changed Everything
Water on the Moon — confirmed. This single discovery influenced every subsequent lunar programme on Earth. NASA's Artemis targets the south pole because of what Chandrayaan-1 found there.
🗺️
70,000+ Images · 3D Lunar Maps
TMC produced the most detailed 3D maps of the lunar surface at the time — data still used by mission planners today. Chandrayaan-1 mapped what Chandrayaan-3 later landed on.
🌏
India as a Serious Space Nation
Before Chandrayaan-1, India was seen as a utilitarian space agency — satellites for weather and communication. After, India was recognised as a deep space science power. Everything changed.
🔭
The Seed of Chandrayaan-3
Chandrayaan-1 targeted the south pole region. Chandrayaan-3 landed there. The scientific thread runs directly from 2008 to 2023 — India spent 15 years understanding what it discovered and then went back to confirm it in person.
Ground Segment

IDSN — India's Deep Space Network

All of Chandrayaan-1's commands and science data — including the historic water discovery — were routed through the Indian Deep Space Network (IDSN) at Byalalu, Karnataka. The IDSN's 18-metre and 32-metre dish antennas tracked Chandrayaan-1 across 400,000 km of space for 312 days without interruption. Every one of the 70,000+ images it sent home passed through these antennas. IDSN is the silent backbone of every ISRO deep space mission.
